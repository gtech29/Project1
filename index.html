Project Overview

With the growing presence of generative technologies in education and the workplace, future educators need to be ready not only to utilize them, but also to critically evaluate their drawbacks, their assumptions and ethical implications. This project examines how teachers and students can use interview simulators, which utilize large language models, both as a part of teacher education coursework and view students as active participants in technological use.

Why This Project

As digital tools become more common in schools and workplaces, future educators will encounter technology not only as teachers, but as professionals being evaluated, interviewed, and supported by automated systems. Many students, however, have limited opportunities to explore how these tools work or to reflect on their strengths and limitations. This project creates a space for students to practice interviewing while also stepping back to consider how technology shapes communication, decision-making, and fairness in education.

What the Interview Simulator Does

The interview simulator presents students with common interview questions they might encounter as future teachers. These include questions about classroom management, teaching philosophy, and working with diverse learners. Students respond as they would in a real interview. The simulator then continues the conversation, allowing students to reflect on the tone, phrasing, and assumptions behind the questions and responses. The goal is not to score students or judge their performance, but to give them a realistic scenario they can learn from..

How Students Use It

Students engage with the simulator as part of their coursework through a simple, guided process: Participate in a simulated teaching interview Review the questions and feedback they received Reflect on what felt helpful, unclear, or unrealistic Discuss how technology compares to human feedback in interview settings These activities help students slow down and think critically about their experience, rather than focusing only on “getting the right answer.”
